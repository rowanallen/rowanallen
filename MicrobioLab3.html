Identification of bacterial species based on observed colonial morphology when cultured on nutrient agar plates, agar slants, and in liquid broth

Elizabeth Allen

BIOL 3150 - Microbiology


Introduction

In this lab exercise, a number of unknown bacterial species were cultured on nutrient agar plates and slants, as well as liquid nutrient broth. Macroscopic observation of the bacterial cultures was used to identify each sample from a given list of possible species.


A preliminary identification of the different bacterial cultures was made based on their morphology when grown on nutrient agar using the streak plate technique. Observed characteristics such as colony shape, texture, and pigmentation of each culture were compared against the existing scientific literature describing each of the possible bacterial species. The five possible bacterial species were Bacillus subtilis, Escherichia coli, Micrococcus luteus, Streptomyces albus, and Kocuria rosea. However, each bacterial sample was given a colour code (green, blue, yellow, pink, or magenta) and the true identity of each sample was not given. These initial predictions were verified by examining the appearance of the same cultures on agar slants and in nutrient broth, and confirming that the appearance of the culture in these preparations matched the known properties of the species that had been hypothesized from earlier observations.


A mixture of Micrococcus luteus and Bacillus subtilis was also inoculated on agar plates, using both the streak plate and pour plate technique. This was done to compare how each of the different inoculation methods affected the growth and appearance of bacterial colonies.


Materials and Methods

The experiment was conducted according to the procedures outlined for “Laboratory 1: Basic Techniques I” in the Laboratory Manual for BIOL 3150 at York University. (Noel et al. 2016)


Results

When the bacteria mixture of Micrococcus luteus and Kocuria rosea was inoculated using the streak plate technique, it was easier to differentiate the colonies of the separate bacteria cultures as distinct from each other. Comparatively, when the mixture was grown on agar using the pour plate techniques, the cultures are difficult to distinguish from each other due to their similar pigmentation and mixing of the cultures throughout the agar.


When grown on agar using the streak plate technique, the bacterial species labeled 'Green' produced colonies that were of medium size, with an off-white colouration and an irregular shape with an undulate margin. The colonies were raised and had a mucoid texture. Using the same technique, the bacteria labeled 'Blue' formed colonies that were small and off-white, with a circular shape and entire margin. The colonies were convex and had a moist texture. The bacteria coded as 'Yellow' produced colonies that were small and had bright yellow pigmentation, with a circular shape and entire margin. The colonies were convex and had a moist texture. The bacteria labeled 'Pink' formed colonies that were small and off-white, with a circular shape and entire margin. The colonies were convex and had a moist texture. Finally, the colonies of the bacteria labeled 'Magenta' were of medium size and had an orange-yellow pigmentation, with a circular shape and entire margin. The colonies were convex and had a moist texture.


When grown in nutrient broth, the bacterial species labeled 'Green' produced a culture with very low turbidity and a clear appearance, and the formation of a pellicle on the surface of the broth. Using the same growth technique in broth, the bacteria labeled 'Blue' produced a culture with uniform fine turbidity and the formation of a sediment. The 'Yellow' bacterial species also produced a uniform fine turbidity in broth, and the presence of a sediment. The bacteria labeled 'Pink' displayed a very low turbidity when cultured in broth, with a clear appearance, and the formation of a pellicle and sediment. Lastly, the bacteria designated as 'Magenta' displayed uniform fine turbidity, and the formation of a pellicle and sediment.


The agar used in this laboratory exercise is well balanced to enable all of the bacterial species to form colonies. However, the use of selective media allows one to create an environment in which only the desired bacterial species are able to survive. For example, by inoculating cells on a nutrient medium containing an antibiotic, one could selectively grow only the cells in a population which had a genetic resistance to that antibiotic.


There are other ways to control the environment in order to selectively promote the growth of desired bacteria. For example, to isolate only anaerobic cells from a mixed culture, one would need to inoculate the bacteria in controlled conditions that contained no oxygen. One published method for growing anaerobic bacteria involves the use of a one-way flask closure that allows air to be removed from the flask during autoclaving, and prevent re-entry of air into the flask after cooling. The flask can then be inoculated by using a sterile needle and syringe to pierce the rubber flask closure, and inject the desired bacteria into the flask (Behbehani et al. 1981).


Conclusion

From the results obtained in Exercise 1, we have determined that the bacteria being observed is Bacillus subtilis. Several factors point to this being the case. The observed bacteria has individual rod-shaped cells, which is consistent with the known properties of B. subtilis (Kobayashi et al. 2012). Furthermore, the bacteria was able to be stained by the crystal violet, indicating that it is Gram-positive, and B. subtilis is known to be a Gram-positive bacteria (Arnaouteli et al. 2016).


In exercise 2, each bacterial species were identified by examining the morphological traits of each colony and comparing them to the known information about each of the possible bacteria. The bacteria species labeled 'Yellow' was identified as Micrococcus luteus. The bright yellow colour of the colonies is the identifying feature of this species, and is due to the presence of a carotenoid pigment in the plasma membrane. The carotenoid pigment associates with membrane lipids, and functions to protect membrane components from oxidation by light (Anwar et al. 1977).


The bacteria species labeled 'Green' was identified as Bacillus subtilis. The undulate shape of the colonies when grown on a streak plate is a notable way to identify this bacteria, as well as its textured and moist surface (Kobayashi et al. 2012). When grown in nutrient broth, this bacteria forms a pellicle at the surface of the broth due to the production of a hydrophobic biofilm. This biofilm contains the protein BslA, which organizes itself into a lattice structure and forms a protective coat at the interface between the air and the broth (Arnaouteli et al. 2016).


The bacteria species labeled as 'Blue' is Escherichia coli. The morphological characteristics of the bacterial colonies were consistent with the existing literature that describes E. Coli as forming off-white, circular colonies with an entire edge and convex elevation (Islam et al. 2014).


For the bacteria species labeled 'Pink', the off-white colouration and effuse form of the colonies on an agar slant match the known appearance of the bacteria Streptomyces albus (Hagvak et al. 2008).This bacteria also forms a pellicle when cultured in nutrient broth, which is an important factor in identifying this bacteria. The formation of the pellicle is due to the hydrophobic sheath composed of rodin and chaplin proteins that covers the aerial hyphae of S. albus (Bibb et al. 2012). Although streptomyces do not sporulate in liquid culture, they still undergo physiological differentiation comparable to that in solid cultures and form aerial mycelia (Manteca et al. 2018).


For the bacteria species labeled 'Magenta', the orange pigment of the culture suggests that this is Kocuria rosea. Colonies of K. rosea are known to have a reddish colour due to the presence of bacteriorubin, a type of carotenoid pigment, hence its species name meaning “rose-coloured” (Chattopadyay et al. 1997).
