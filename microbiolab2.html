Microbial Genetics and Transduction of Foreign DNA into Bacteria

Rowan Allen

BIOL 3150 - Microbiology


Introduction

In the context of microbial genetics, a competent cell is one that has the ability to take in foreign DNA from its environment by horizontal gene transfer. Competency is a temporary state, and may be induced in the laboratory by heat shock, or in nature by the expression of genes that induce the physiological state of competence. (Chan et al. 2013)


In certain plants, natural competency can be induced by tissue damage. In response to a wound, plants initiate rapid cell division and release phytohormones which have been shown to predispose cells to transformation by Agrobacterium tumefaciens. (Stachel et al. 1985) Only cells in a relatively un-differentiated state, such as mesophyll or root pericycle cells, are capable of natural competency due to their developmental plasticity. (Sangwan et al. 1992)


Foreign DNA can be introduced into competent E. coli by either electroporation or chemical transformation. In electroporation, cells are briefly exposed to an electric current, which creates holes in the plasma membrane which negatively-charged foreign DNA can pass through. (Gonzales et al. 2013) Chemically competent cells are treated with calcium chloride. The Ca2+ ion creates pores in the membrane and masks the negative charge of the foreign DNA, allowing it to move into the cell. (Chan et al. 2013) Electroporation requires a low salt environment; if the conductivity of the cell sample is too high, electrical arcing will occur and ruin the experiment. (Tu et al. 2016) For this reason, the two methods cannot be combined.


Traditionally, E. coli has not been considered naturally competent. However, recent research suggests that E. coli may undergo physiological changes that allow transformation to occur, thereby acquiring natural competency. In the protocol developed by Sun et al., high Ca2+ concentrations or special conditions were not required in order for E. coli to be transformable on plates at 37 C. (Sun et al. 2006) Notably, it was shown that E. coli was not transformable on plates containing chloramphenicol. Since chloramphenicol inhibits protein synthesis, it is likely that the lack of competency was due to the absence of proteins required for DNA uptake. (Otto et al. 2002) The implication that certain proteins are necessary for the transformation process suggests that E. coli has specific genes for inducing competency. In fact, E. coli is known to have genes homologous to those used for genetic transformation in Haemophilus influenzae and Neisseria gonorrhoeae, which further supports the hypothesis that the bacterium may be capable of natural competency. (Palchevskiy et al. 2006)


Results

The results of this experiment suggest that the transformation of E. coli by heat shock was a success. The bacteria cultured on the LB + tetracycline agar were able to grow on the medium and form colonies, although the number of colonies was less than on the control plates lacking antibiotic. From these results, we can conclude that at least some of the E. coli bacteria were able to be transformed and uptake the antibiotic resistance gene. The control plates that contained only LB without any antibiotic were used to show that the E. coli cultures were alive and able to reproduce on the agar. This was done so that if the bacteria failed to grow on the LB + tetracycline plates, we could deduce that the addition of tetracycline was the factor that inhibited their growth, since the presence of the antibiotic was the only difference from the control plates.


Question: What would we expect to see if tetracycline was omitted from all of the plates?

If tetracycline was omitted from all of the plates, we would expect to see abundant E. coli growth on all plates. This would make it impossible to distinguish between bacteria that had been successfully transformed and those which had not, as there would be no difference in their growth. However, if ampicillin was used on the plates instead of tetracycline, we would still be able to differentiate between the bacteria that had been transformed with the foreign DNA and those which had not. This is because the pBR322 plasmid contains two antibiotic resistance genes, one for tetracycline and one for ampicillin. (Watson 1988) Therefore, our results would not change if we substituted ampicillin for tetracycline in our experiment.
