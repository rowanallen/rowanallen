Description and comparison of coliform bacteria isolated from commercial ground meat stored at 4 °C and 37 °C grown on EMB and nutrient agar

Elizabeth Allen

BIOL 3150 - Microbiology


Materials and Methods

The experiment was conducted according to the procedures outlined for “Laboratory 4, Exercise 2: Food Microbiology, Part 1” and “Lab 5, Exercise 3: Food Microbiology, Part 2” in the Laboratory Manual for BIOL 3150 at York University. (Noel et al. 2016)


Results

By conducting a Gram stain on a sample of bacteria taken from the ground meat, we can identify the bacteria as Gram-negative due to its pink coloration. Observation under the microscope reveals that the bacteria is rod-shaped. When grown on agar at 4 °C and 37 °C for the same length of time, a greater number of colonies were observed on the plates kept at 37 °C. When the bacteria were subjected to the oxidase test, the result was negative. Additionally, the result of the catalase test was positive. Based on these properties, we can classify the bacteria as a coliform, likely originating in the digestive tract of an animal and exposed to the ground meat during processing. No color change was observed when the bacteria were grown on EMB agar, indicating that the bacteria was not a lactose-fermenting species such as E. Coli, but rather a non-lactose-fermenting coliform such as those in the genus Salmonella.


Discussion

Foodborne pathogens present a serious risk to public health, and the processing of meat is especially vulnerable to bacterial contamination. The bacteria Escherichia Coli is a serious concern in food safety due to the ability of some strains to cause illness and death in humans. (Edberg et al. 2012) For this reason, meat products are tested for E. Coli and other coliforms. Coliform bacteria are defined as Gram-negative, rod-shaped bacteria with the ability to ferment lactose, and are found in the digestive tracts of animals. (Kaper et al. 2004) While coliform bacteria themselves are often not disease-causing, their presence indicates that meat has been contaminated with feces. (Cohen et al. 1973)


In this experiment, we tested ground raw meat, and canned processed meat, for the presence of coliform bacteria. The ground meat was divided into two samples, one of which was stored at 4 °C and the other stored at 37 °C. The number of colonies that were grown from the 37 °C meat sample was considerably higher than those grown from the 4 °C meat sample. Our results are consistent with what would be expected based on existing knowledge about coliform bacteria, which are able to grow ideally at a temperature of 35-37 °C. (Raghubeer et al. 1990) Based on the properties of the observed bacteria, we can classify it as a coliform, as it was found to be Gram-negative, rod-shaped, and oxidase negative. It likely originated in the digestive tract of an animal and was exposed to the ground meat during processing. No color change was observed when the bacteria were grown on EMB agar, indicating that the bacteria was not a lactose-fermenting species such as E. Coli, but rather a non-lactose-fermenting coliform such as those in the genus Salmonella. (Leininger et al. 2001)


The canned meat (SPAM) was found to have no bacterial contamination. This is due to the canning process, which contains several steps designed to kill bacteria. First, air is evacuated from the can and the can is sealed. Next, the can is thermally processed in a hydrostatic cooker which heats the cans to an internal temperature of 212 °C. (Grispoldi et al. 2019) This processing ensures that all bacteria are killed, so the meat is able to last for years inside the can without spoiling.


There are several tests for identifying coliform bacteria. The conventional method for testing for coliform bacteria involves culturing on EMB agar and noting whether colonies with an iridescent sheen grow on the plate. Alternatively, violet red bile agar can be used as a test medium. If coliform bacteria are present, purple-red colonies will grow on the agar, surrounded by a zone of precipitated bile acids. (Leclerq et al. 2002) Another method for coliform detection is the LST-MUG assay, which uses 4-methylumbelliferyl-beta-D-glucuronide (MUG) incorporated into lauryl tryptose (LST) broth. This assay depends on the production of beta-glucuronidase by E. Coli, which cleaves MUG and releases 4-methylumbelliferone as a byproduct. This byproduct fluoresces when exposed to UV light at 365 nm, and therefore fluorescence indicates the presence of E. Coli. (Moberg 1985)
